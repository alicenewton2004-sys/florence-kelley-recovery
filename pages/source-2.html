Source 02 — Documentary Evidence

Hull House Maps and Papers

New York: Thomas Y. Crowell, 1895 — Collectively authored, data-driven argument for political intervention.

Home›Source 2: Hull House Maps & Papers

The Hull House Maps and Papers (1895) is one of the earliest examples of systematic social survey methodology in the United States. Produced collaboratively by residents of Hull House — including Florence Kelley — it combines essays, statistical charts, and colour-coded neighbourhood maps to document wages, occupations, and housing conditions across Chicago's immigrant communities. As a rhetorical document, its most powerful feature is precisely this: it translates observation into argument by rendering the invisible conditions of working-class women and children legible in a form that demanded official attention.

Data as Argument: The Rhetorical Function of the Maps

The wage and ethnicity maps are the most rhetorically powerful elements of the collection. By colour-coding streets according to weekly earnings and ethnic origin, they make visible two things that the culture of the period worked hard to keep separate: the relationship between ethnicity and poverty, and the concentration of that poverty among women and children employed in sweated trades.

This is not disinterested social science. The maps are designed to produce a particular response in a particular kind of reader: the legislative or policy audience whose attention Kelley was trying to secure. They argue, visually and statistically, that the conditions documented are systematic — not individual failures of character or effort — and that they therefore demand institutional, legislative remedy.

Collective Authorship and Archival Erasure

The most analytically significant feature of the Hull House Maps and Papers for this recovery project is its collective authorship. Kelley is not credited as the sole author of any single chapter; she is one voice among many, embedded in a collectively produced document. This reflects her actual method — she understood research and advocacy as collaborative, structural practices, not individual acts of genius.

But the archive rewards singular authorship. A collectively authored document that credits no primary author risks being attributed to no one in particular, or to the institution rather than the people within it. Kelley's absence as individually credited author is therefore both accurate and distorting: accurate because the work was genuinely collaborative; distorting because the archive's preference for singular attribution makes collective labour harder to trace and credit.

Shaped Silences: The Limits of the Survey

The Hull House Maps and Papers documents one neighbourhood, in one moment, with the methodological tools available in the 1890s. The maps cannot show what happened to the people they document after the survey was conducted, who was excluded from the survey's frame, or what the conditions documented felt like to those living within them. These are what Sara Ahmed might call 'shaped silences': absences that are not accidental but structural, produced by the limits of a particular methodology and a particular moment.

Data as argument: the wage and ethnicity maps render conditions of working-class women and children legible in a form that demanded official attention. Yet there are limits. These are shaped silences, not accidental ones.— Project analysis
