Original Statement — Written at the Semester's Start

I Am Often Guilty of Feeling Like a Bad Feminist

I do not reject feminism, nor do I doubt its necessity, and I certainly believe in promoting it, but I constantly grapple with how to live it. My gender has never denied me an education or forced me to choose between ambition and acceptance — not yet, anyway. Although I consider myself a feminist, at times I feel extremely disconnected from the movement and view it from afar, rather than inhabiting it fully. My distance is not born from indifference, but it seems to be a form of protection that has so far left me relatively unscathed.

Feminism first entered my conscience as an eight-year-old girl in History class when learning about the Suffragettes. I was mesmerised by their relentlessness in the face of male oppression and willingness to put their lives on the line for a cause so much greater than themselves. They seemed somewhat mythical to me — like heroes you would never meet. In a way, that is how I felt about feminism: a movement I studied, respected, and aspired toward, rather than something I instinctively embodied.

Childhood and the Illusion of Protection

For much of my childhood, gender did not register to me as a meaningful barrier. I grew up in the English countryside as one of four siblings, competing for the boys' soccer team until I was eleven and never once doubting my belonging there. For me, personal identity was mainly understood through recognising what and who I loved, not through gendered expectations or social limitation.

That shelter shifted when I entered an all-girls boarding school at eleven. Gender became both more apparent and more visible. The absence of boys created a sense of safety I had not previously known how to articulate. And yet gender expectations presented themselves in other forms: competitive sport was framed as unfeminine; eating disorders were a frequent and troubling presence. At the time, I understood this primarily as a response to academic pressure. Only later did I recognise how much was shaped by anxiety around male perception of the female body — an imagined gaze that lingered even in the absence of men.

Finance, the Golf Course, and Structural Awakening

My academic life as an economics major widened the gap between myself and a felt connection to feminism. In a gender economics course, I found myself reducing many women to statistics — creating an often abstract engagement with inequality that did not seem to implicate my own future within those systems. Feminism, for me, largely existed as an analytical framework rather than a lived practice of immediate concern.

That naivety was unsettled by a single moment during an investment banking recruitment event last spring. A male associate proudly announced that nearly ninety percent of deals were closed on the golf course. The comment struck me with unexpected force — not because it was framed as exclusionary (it was not), but because it was offered as a blunt matter of fact. For the first time, I confronted the possibility that gendered exclusion does not always appear as denial or discrimination, but as absence: an absence so subtle that relationships are built, trust is established, and opportunity quietly circulates without women present at all.

What unsettled me most was not the exclusion itself but how easily it could be dismissed as incidental rather than structural. If women are absent from the majority of deal closings from the outset, how are they expected to progress at the same rate?

Theoretical Frameworks

Sarah Ahmed's argument in Living a Feminist Life — that feminism is a practice of noticing how power operates in everyday life — helped me understand that my belated awakening was not evidence of inadequate feminist commitment but of the particular conditions of my formation. Feminist consciousness emerged not from a single dramatic injustice but from cumulative recognition.

Adrienne Rich's Notes Toward a Politics of Location helped me understand why this trajectory matters: there is no neutral standpoint. As a White, European woman educated at strong institutions and insulated from many material consequences of gendered and racial oppression, I need to acknowledge how my position shapes what I notice, when I notice it, and what feels urgent. This Bridge Called My Back (Moraga and Anzaldúa) deepened this further: feminist movements, and feminist scholarship, can reproduce hierarchies even as they seek to dismantle them.

Linda Alcoff's reminder that silence is not a neutral alternative to speaking gave me the clearest impetus. I no longer wanted to witness feminist rhetoric. I wanted to write my own.

Reflection — How This Project Changed the Statement

Florence Kelley and What She Taught Me

When I chose Florence Kelley as my subject, I understood the resonance intellectually: her feminism was structural rather than dramatic, oriented toward the everyday conditions that disadvantaged women and children rather than toward overt acts of discrimination. That description mapped onto my own experience — the golf course comment, the informal exclusions of finance, the gendered dynamics of social life that are rarely framed as exclusionary precisely because they are so normalised.

What I did not fully appreciate at the start was how much Kelley's method — not just her subject — would challenge and reshape my sense of my own position as a researcher.

How Kelley Reshaped My Positionality

Kelley understood that making the invisible legible required not only the right argument but the right form. Data, cartography, testimony, letters: each source she left behind is a different strategy for translating private experience into public and political terms. That range of forms is itself a lesson in what feminist rhetorical practice requires.

As a researcher, I came to this project with anticipation rather than lived experience of professional exclusion. Kelley's example showed me that this is not disqualifying. What mattered was the seriousness of the inquiry and the commitment to making what was invisible legible — regardless of whether the conditions documented were her own.

The Archive and My Limitations

Kelley's shaped silences mirror the limits of my own research position. I risk preserving the argument while erasing the experience. Kelley's example is a constant reminder to read against that risk.

Structural Awakening, Then and Now

Kelley's feminist awakening was itself gradual and structural — emerging through exposure to conditions she chose to investigate. Her trajectory authorises a feminism that begins with noticing, rather than with immediate personal oppression. That is the feminism I am trying to inhabit.

Finance and Informal Power

The golf course comment finds a precise parallel in Kelley's analysis of the sweated trades: conditions so normalised they were barely visible until mapped, quantified, and argued before those with the power to change them.

Speaking from a Location

Kelley's location shaped her research, her arguments, and her method. Mine is different but no less specific, and no less shaping. This project has made me more attentive to what my location enables me to see and what it risks making invisible.

At the semester's start, I described myself as an 'inactive feminist' rather than a bad one. Working with Kelley has suggested a more useful formulation: I am a feminist in the process of developing the tools to act on what I notice. The noticing is already political. The question now is what to do with it.

Especially since I am interning this summer at the firm where a male associate told a room that ninety percent of deals close on a golf course — I want to ensure that I leave complacency behind. I understand now what I believe in. I am beginning to understand how to act on it.
